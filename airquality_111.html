🗽 MN11(East Harlem) Air Quality Issue Related Dashboard

Air quality directly affects human health, particularly respiratory function. Breathing in unhealthy levels of fine particles (PM 2.5) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) can increase the risk of asthma, cardiovascular diseases, and other health issues (NYC Department of Health). Air pollution levels differ across neighborhoods in New York City. Low-income neighborhoods, such as Hunts Point, face higher air pollution levels compared to high-income neighborhoods. Areas like South Bronx, Northern Manhattan, and Northern Brooklyn also have the highest rate of respiratory hospitalization due to the PM 2.5 (NYU School of Global Public Health, n.d.). Additionally, neighborhoods of color are exposed to higher air pollution, like emissions from vehicles, compared to neighborhoods of white, due to the location of highways, waste routes, and heavy-duty fleets (NYC Mayor’s Office of Climate and Environmental Justice, n.d.).

MN11 Air Quality Complaints Received & Government Action*

We used DEP's MMR indicator that 99% of air quality complaints not requiring access to premises are responded to within 7 days. Giving DEP the benefit of the doubt, we are assuming 99% of most air quality complaints are therefore resolved in 7 days.

Citywide Total Air Quality Violations Issued*

This chart presents the total number of air quality violations issued annually, based on MMR reports. While community-level data is unavailable, this citywide metric provides insight into enforcement activity. Note: DEP summons are issued for multiple environmental issues, only a fraction of which relate to air quality. Nonetheless, we are using them as a proxy for government actions.

Results*

The fine particle level (PM 2.5) in East Harlem has dropped from 7.13 µg/m³ in 2019 to 6.16 µg/m³ in 2022, while nitrogen dioxide level (NO2) has also experienced a significant drop from 18.8 mcg/ m³ to 16.4 mcg/ m³ in 2023. During that time, the government enhanced environmental protection citywide and significantly increased its efforts in addressing air quality complaints. The number of summonses citywide issued on air quality-related violations nearly quadrupled from 2021 to 2024.
However, residential satisfaction in East Harlem has trended downward. The satisfaction rate was reduced by half from 2017 to 2023, and it was even below the level in 2008, indicating that air quality issues have become more serious in residents’ minds. In conclusion, although the government has taken significant steps in controlling air quality violations and addressing air quality complaints, residents still are not satisfied with the air quality level in their neighborhood.

*Caveats: None of our data sets are ideal, and many are not comparable temporally or substantively. For example, while far from ideal, we chose to use the City’s Community Board Districts as a proxy for neighborhoods since much of the city’s data and service delivery infrastructure is organized along these lines. Measures of community sentiment are approximated from resident surveys and 311 data, which are imprecise. Some government actions we chose do not capture the full scope of government actions being taken, or are not publicly available by community district. (For example DEP summonses may address noise, air pollution, or a host of other topics.  In another example, while 311 complaints are a valid proxy indicator of some neighborhood sentiment, they do have some shortcomings as a truly representative indicator. In yet another case the government’s self-reported statistic about the very high percentage of complaints “resolved” begs further detail). But the project’s purpose is to suggest what a neighborhood dashboard might look like, even with these imprecisions, and to suggest possible pathways to creating an effective one for residents, policymakers, and government alike. Therefore, these are expressed as “Results,”  despite the acknowledged shortcomings of the data.
