🗽 MN08(Upper East Side) Sanitation Issue Related Dashboard

Sanitation directly influences resident’s life experiences and health in the neighborhood. Poor sanitation services, such as garbage accumulation and rat control, can be detrimental to the neighborhood environment and lead to serious diseases. To reduce the negative effects brought by sanitation issues, the New York City Department of Health has strictly forbidden inhabitants to throw garbage in the streets since the 1600s (NYC Department of Health, 2023). Although the regulation has made significant progress, sanitation issues remain serious in some areas, reflecting a big disparity in resident’s life experience. Research by NYC Department of Health (2023) indicates that residents in East Harlem experience more trash on streets and sidewalks, and community health workers documented concerns about trash issues, such as overflowing waste bins and inadequate services, which threaten residents’ quality of life.

Results*

Sanitation conditions in the Upper Side didn’t change that much from 2022 to 2024. The number of unsanitary conditions reported from 311 remains at the same level throughout these years. Residential satisfaction in neighborhood cleanliness, garbage pickup, and rat control slightly decreased in these years. However, this rate is still above average compared to other places.
Citywide, the government made a significant effort to improve sanitation conditions, especially in illegal dumping control and needle removal. Summonses for illegal dumping increased from 184 in 2021 to 721 in 2024, and the number of removed needles rose from 32,252 to 83,307.
Overall, residents seem to be relatively more satisfied with government progress and the results from government actions.

*Caveats: None of our data sets are ideal, and many are not comparable temporally or substantively. For example, while far from ideal, we chose to use the City’s Community Board Districts as a proxy for neighborhoods since much of the city’s data and service delivery infrastructure is organized along these lines. Measures of community sentiment are approximated from resident surveys and 311 data, which are imprecise. Some government actions we chose do not capture the full scope of government actions being taken, or are not publicly available by community district. (For example DEP summonses may address noise, air pollution, or a host of other topics.  In another example, while 311 complaints are a valid proxy indicator of some neighborhood sentiment, they do have some shortcomings as a truly representative indicator. In yet another case the government’s self-reported statistic about the very high percentage of complaints “resolved” begs further detail). But the project’s purpose is to suggest what a neighborhood dashboard might look like, even with these imprecisions, and to suggest possible pathways to creating an effective one for residents, policymakers, and government alike. Therefore, these are expressed as “Results,”  despite the acknowledged shortcomings of the data.
