🗽 MN11(East Harlem) Homeless Issue Related Dashboard

Over 80,000 people in New York City rely on shelters each night, and this group includes a large portion of women and children (Coalition for the Homeless, 2024). Unemployment, high housing costs, and lack of affordable housing are the reasons which contribute to the homelessness problem. Research by the Urban Institute (2023) indicates that areas with low-income rates or high burden rates usually have more shelters for homelessness, and the homelessness rate is relatively high in these areas. The government should increase affordable housing, improve mental health services, and provide job training to homeless people, ultimately reducing the portion of homelessness in the city (National Alliance to End Homelessness, 2023).

Results*

Although the number of homeless encampments reported through 311 dropped from 1,033 in 2022 to 475 in 2024, residents still lack confidence about homeless issues in their neighborhoods. Satisfaction rates of public housing and homeless services are only 9% and 13.3%, respectively.
Citywide, the government made progress by providing shelters for homeless people and those in need. The number of public housing placements through the government's HOME-STAT program increased from 5,453 in 2021 to 11,503 in 2024. However, citywide statistics from the government show that the number of estimated unsheltered homeless individuals increased from 3,439 in 2021 to 4,120 in 2023.

*Caveats: None of our data sets are ideal, and many are not comparable temporally or substantively. For example, while far from ideal, we chose to use the City’s Community Board Districts as a proxy for neighborhoods since much of the city’s data and service delivery infrastructure is organized along these lines. Measures of community sentiment are approximated from resident surveys and 311 data, which are imprecise. Some government actions we chose do not capture the full scope of government actions being taken, or are not publicly available by community district. (For example DEP summonses may address noise, air pollution, or a host of other topics.  In another example, while 311 complaints are a valid proxy indicator of some neighborhood sentiment, they do have some shortcomings as a truly representative indicator. In yet another case the government’s self-reported statistic about the very high percentage of complaints “resolved” begs further detail). But the project’s purpose is to suggest what a neighborhood dashboard might look like, even with these imprecisions, and to suggest possible pathways to creating an effective one for residents, policymakers, and government alike. Therefore, these are expressed as “Results,”  despite the acknowledged shortcomings of the data.
